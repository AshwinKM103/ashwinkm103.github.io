Leadership & Extracurriculars

The parts of my work that happen away from a terminal tend to feed back into the research. I came up through policy debate and the World Scholar's Cup, where the useful skill was holding two readings of the same question at once and reading disagreement as a signal rather than friction — a habit I still lean on when a result refuses to behave. At IISc I founded Nexus, a SciTech initiative that builds entry points into AI, robotics, and automation through workshops and student-run competitions, and I have coordinated sponsorship and events for the Pravega festival. What those roles taught me is that participation is mostly a question of culture: whether a newcomer feels free to ask an obvious question usually decides who stays.

The same concern shows up in how I think about deployment. When we took MentorX to roughly fifty students at a Kendriya Vidyalaya near IISc, and built supplementary learning videos to go with it, the lesson was that a system only matters if it holds up once it leaves the setting it was built in. I have also presented this work as a lead speaker at the International Conference on Applied AI and Scientific Machine Learning. Across all of it I keep returning to the same standard: technical work should answer to a specific human need, not just produce an output.
